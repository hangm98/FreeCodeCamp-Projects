Brief history of Ho Chi Minh

President Ho Chi Minh also known as Uncle Ho was born in 1980 in Hoang Tru in rural Vietnam. His birthname was Nguyen Sinh Cung, then it was changed later to Nguyen Tat Thanh by his father. Starting from June 1911 Nguyen Tat Thanh officially began a new journey to find a path for the national liberation struggle that was at a standstill at that time. Throughout his life, he had worked as a kitchen helper, a pasty chef, a jounarlist, a revolutionalist, a politician, and many more jobs before he became the President of the Democratic Republic of Vietnam. Uncle Ho dedicated 58 years of his life fighting for the dependent of Vietname and finally entered his enternal rest on September 2nd, 1969. The life of President Ho Chi Minh is a pure and beautiful life of a great communist, an outstanding national hero, an eminent international soldier, who fought tirelessly and devoted all my life for the Fatherland, for the people, and for ideals.

Timeline of Ho Chi Minh's Journey

1911 - French Steamer Journey

Ho Chi Minh started working on a French steamer, the Amiral de Latouche-Tréville while using the alias Van Ba. This was the beginning of his journey travelling around the world.

1912 - Time in The United States

The ship Ho Chi Minh was working on docked in the USA in 1912. From then and until 1913, he lived in Boston, NY and worked as a baker.

1920 - Representative To The Congress of Tours of The Socialist Party of France

In December 1920, Ho Chi Minh became a representative to the Congress of Tours of the Socialist Party of France, voted for the Third International and was a founding member of the French Communist Party. Taking a position in the Colonial Committee of the party, he tried to draw his comrades' attention towards people in French colonies including Indochina, but his efforts were often unsuccessful.

1923 - From Paris to Moscow

Ho Chi Minh left Paris for Moscow carrying a passport with the name Chen Vang, a Chinese merchant, where he was employed by the Comintern, studied at the Communist University of the Toilers of the East. He then participated in the Fifth Comintern Congress in June 1924.

1924 - From Moscow to Guangzhou

Ho Chi Minh arrived in Canton (present-day Guangzhou), China in November 1924 using the name Ly Thuy. From then to 1926, he organized "Youth Education Classes" and occasionally gave socialist lectures to Vietnamese revolutionary young people living in Canton at the Whampoa Military Academy. These young people would become the seeds of a new revolutionary, pro-communist movement in Vietnam several years later. According to William Duiker.

1930 - Chairing a meeting with representatives from two Vietnamese Communist Parties

In Hong Kong in early 1930, Ho Chi Minh chaired a meeting with representatives from two Vietnamese Communist parties in order to merge them into a unified organization, the Communist Party of Vietnam. This leaded to his arrest by the British; Furthermore, to reduce French pressure for extradition of Ho Chi Minh, he was falsely annouced dead.

1933 - Return to Moscow

The British quietly released Ho Chi Minh in January 1933. He moved to the Soviet Union and in Moscow studied and taught at the Lenin Institute.

1941 - Return to Vietnam

In 1941, Ho Chi Minh returned to Vietnam to lead the Việt Minh independence movement.

1945 - A Successful Revolution

Ho Chi Minh's Việt Minh (League for the Independence of Vietnam) launched revolution against French colonial rule in Vietnam, on August 14, 1945. With the help of Japanese troops, Vietname along with Laos and Cambodia declared independent. On September 1945, Ho Chi Minh declares an independent North Vietnam and models his declaration on the American Declaration of Independence of 1776 in an (unsuccessful) effort to win the support of the United States. Following the August Revolution (1945) organized by the Việt Minh, Hồ Chí Minh became Chairman of the Provisional Government (Premier of the Democratic Republic of Vietnam) and issued a Proclamation of Independence of the Democratic Republic of Vietnam. Ho Chi Minh became the 1st President of the Democratic Republic of Vietnam.

1946 - The Beginning of The Indochina War

Ho Chi Minh rejects a French proposal granting Vietnam limited self-government and the Viet Minh begins a guerrilla war against the French.

1950 - The Allies

The People's Republic of China and the Soviet Union formally recognized the communist Democratic Republic of Vietnam and both began to supply economic and military aid to communist resistance fighters within the country. Assisted by the Soviet Union and the newly Communist China, the Viet Minh stepped up their offensive against French outposts in Vietnam. Ho Chi Minh became the Chairman of the Central Committee of the Workers' Party of Vietnam.

1954 - The 1954 Geneva Accords and The Battle of Dien Bien Phu

French troops were humiliated in defeat by Viet Minh forces at Dien Bien Phu. The defeat solidified the end of French rule in Indochina. The 1954 Geneva Accords established North and South Vietnam with the 17th parallel as the dividing line. The agreement also stipulated that elections were to be held within two years to unify Vietnam under a single democratic government. Unfortunately, these elections never happen.

1956 - The Idea of Overthrowing The South Vietnamese Government

Hồ Chí Minh became the First Secretary of the Central Committee of the Workers' Party of Vietnam. He then presented the idea of overthrowing the South Vietnamese government was presented at a politburo meeting.

1959 - Confirming a "People's War" on The South

Ho Chi Minh began urging the Politburo to send aid to the Việt Cộng in South Vietnam and a "people's war" on the South was approved.

1960 - Creation of The National Liberation Front of South Vietnam

To counter the accusation that North Vietnam was violating the Geneva Accord, the independence of the Việt Cộng was stressed in Communist propaganda. North Vietnam created the National Liberation Front of South Vietnam in December 1960 as a "united front", or political branch of the Viet Cong intended to encourage the participation of non-Communists.

1967 - High-Profile Conference

Ho Chi Minh and most of the Politburo of the Communist Party met in a high-profile conference where they concluded the war had fallen into a stalemate.

1968 - Tet Offensive

The Tet Offensive began, encompassing a combined assault of Viet Minh and North Vietnamese armies. Attacks were carried out in more than 100 cities and outposts across South Vietnam, including Hue and Saigon, and the U.S. Embassy was invaded. The effective, bloody attacks shocked U.S. officials and marked a turning point in the war and the beginning of a gradual U.S. withdrawal from the region.

1969 - The Mourning

With the outcome of the Vietnam War still in question, Ho Chi Minh died of heart failure at his home in Hanoi at 9:47 on the morning of 2 September 1969; he was 79 years old.
